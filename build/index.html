Can you win 3-card monte?

How a simple card game can separate skill from probability

By: Jithin George, Alex Fang, Richard Yu

You walk up to a busy city corner and spot a lone street-magician with three cards on a table. Interested, you ask him what magic could possibly come out of these cards. He flips over one of the cards and shows you a red queen of hearts.

He proposes to you that if you can keep track of this queen of hearts (Q♥) you’ll win a prize.

Confident, you agree to the terms and in an instant he flips the cards over and shuffles the three cards multiple times, and stops, leaving you to select which card is the queen.

The wikipedia page for three-card monte commonly refers it as a “short con” where the magician would hire accomplices, or shills, to rig the game.

This simulation aims to show how even a winning a fair game is random chance.

So here’s the question:
How relevant is skill in winning this game as the number of shuffles the magician makes increases?

Game Setup

In a typical setup, three cards are laid out on a table, one of which is the “money card”, in this case Q♥, that if kept track of rewards a prize.

Selecting one of the other two cards means you lose your bet.

Let’s say the two loser cards are Q♠, Q♣, and as previously mentioned, the “money card” is the Q♥.

So the game setup looks like this:

Once you see the starting position of the cards, they are flipped over to conceal their faces. The magician proceeds to shuffle, or swap the position of any two of the three cards, and it is the player’s job to select the Q♥ when he finishes.

Theory

Skill in this game can be represented by a probability ppp that you successfully track a shuffle of two cards. That is,

the probability that you know the exact position of the winner card after the shuffle takes place.
